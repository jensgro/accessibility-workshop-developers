Why should we care about accessibility?

We have ethical, commercial and legal responsibilities to make sure we are inclusive in all areas of our business - including our digital products.

In 2018, a Australian Bureau of Statistics Survey estimated that there were 4.4 million (17.7%) Australians with disability.

The 2013, a New Zealand Disability Survey estimated that there were 1.1 million (24%) New Zealanders with disability.

What are our legal responsibilities?

Australia

Disability Discrimination Act 1992:

“Companies must ensure that people with disabilities have the same fundamental rights to access information and services as others in the community.”

Australian Human Rights Commission:

“All websites and web resources must comply with WCAG 2.0 to a minimum of AA-Level conformance.”

In Australia, all public-facing digital products are:

  • Required to comply with the DDA 1992
  • Expected to conform to WCAG 2.0 AA

New Zealand

In New Zealand, all public-facing digital products are required to comply with The Human Rights Act 1993, and The Human Rights Amendment Act 2001, and cannot discriminate on basis of disability.

Human Rights Commission

“Human rights are what people need to live with dignity and enjoy freedom. All people have human rights, including all people with disabilities.”

Have there been any legal cases?

There have been numerous complaints filed with the Australian Human Rights Commission over the years. In some cases, these have gone through legal processes to be resolved.

The first successful legal challenge in the world was in Australia - “Maguire v SOCOG” in 1999.

The most recent, high profile case was “Gisele Mesnage vs Coles”, which was settled in 2015.

What is WCAG

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG), is a world-wide standard for web accessibility. The guidelines define how to make web content more accessible to people with disabilities.

Within the guidelines, there are 78 Success Criteria that can be used to test websites and applications to determine their level of accessibility.

Each criteria is defined as either:

  • Level “A”: have the greatest impact on a broad range of user populations.
  • Level “AA”: have a high impact for some groups.
  • Level “AAA”: have an impact for very specific groups.

In order for websites and applications to conform to WCAG 2.1 AA, they must meet all “A” and “AA” Success Criteria, which means 50 criteria in total.

Our aim is to ensure that all IAG public-facing digital products:

  • Are accessible to the widest possible audiences.
  • Conform to WCAG 2.1 AA.

The aim of this series of workshops it to demonstrate:

  • The importance of meeting these success criteria.
  • How to build accessible websites.
  • How to test the accessibility of websites.
