What is digital accessibility?

A focus on ensuring the accessibility of digital products and services:

  • Websites and web applications.
  • Native mobile applications.
  • Digital documents (such as PDFs).
  • Email.

What’s the aim of Digital Accessibility?

To ensure that all IAG digital products are accessible to the widest possible audience - including individuals with disabilities.

What does the Accessibility Principal do?

  • Accessibility awareness sessions.
  • Accessibility training workshops.
  • Rollout of accessibility testing tool.
  • Ongoing accessibility compliance audits.
  • Advice and support.

Why should we care about accessibility?

Legal imperatives

In Australia, all of IAG’s public-facing digital products:

  • Are required to comply with the Disability Discrimination Act 1992.
  • Must conform to WCAG 2.1 AA.

In New Zealand, all of IAG’s public-facing digital products:

  • Are required to comply with the The Human Rights Act 1993.
  • Are required to comply with the Human Rights Amendment Act 2001.

Commercial incentives

How many people have some form of disability?

  • Around 17.7% Australians.
  • Around 24% New Zealanders.

However, it is well-known that these estimates are low, as many people do not identify themselves as having some form of disability.

Regardless of the exact percentages, can we afford to alienate any number of potential or existing customers by not providing accessible products and services?

Reputation incentives

In 2015, the legal case “Gisele Mesnage vs Coles” was settled out of court.

While there is a financial risk associated with legal actions like this, the possible damage to organisation’s reputation is a far greater risk.

“It takes many good deeds to build a good reputation, and only one bad one to lose it.”

Benjamin Franklin

What is WCAG?

As we saw before, all of IAG’s public-facing digital products “must conform to WCAG 2.1 AA”. But what does this mean?

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG), is the world-wide standard for web accessibility.

WCAG is the standard for web content accessibility. It explains how to make web content more accessible to people with disabilities.

The latest version of the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines is Version 2.1.

Within the WCAG guidelines, all key criteria are categorised as either:

  • 20 level “A” - broadest audiences
  • 30 level “AA”
  • 28 level “AAA” - niche audiences

To be “AA” compliant, sites must meet all “A” and “AA” Success Criteria.

This means that websites and web apps must comply with all 50 of these Success Criteria.

Auditing digital products

All websites, web apps and native apps need to be regularly audited to determine whether they conform to “WCAG 2.1 AA”.

This can be done by either in-house or external accessibility experts.

Accessibility audits can be conducted on:

  • Entire applications.
  • Specific customer processes.
  • Single screens.
  • Individual components.

Audits can be conducted at any stage in the process:

  • Design wireframes or prototypes.
  • Apps in the early stages of development.
  • Apps that are ready for deployment.
  • Apps that are already live.

The general rule regarding accessibility reviews and audits is “the earlier the better”.

The aim is to try to identify potential issues before code is written, or at least before a site is deployed.

Accessibility audits should provide:

  • A list of all identified errors
  • An indication as to how to fix the error.
  • An indication of the impact of each error.

Each error should include one of four levels of impact.

  • Critical.
  • High.
  • Medium.
  • Low.

Critical impact

These errors can significantly interfere with a person’s use of the page. In most cases, they cannot proceed with a task.

Critical impact errors must be addressed before any website or application goes live.

High impact

These errors mean that there will be some groups of people with disabilities who are not able to use the site or a section of the site at all.

High impact errors should be addressed as soon as possible with the next release.

Medium impact

These errors may increase the amount of time a person with a disability takes to complete a task, or cause the user frustration/confusion.

Low impact

These errors could affect users and should be addressed to fully conform with WCAG 2.1 AA.

Types of disabilities

From a digital accessibility perspective, are four main types of disability:

  • Visual
  • Auditory
  • Motor Skill
  • Cognitive

1. Visual

A. Low Vision:

  • Macular degeneration
  • Glaucoma
  • Diabetic retinopathy
  • Cataract

B. Colour Vision Deficiencies:

  • Protanopia (red deficiencies)
  • Deuteranopia (green deficiencies)
  • Tritanopia (blue deficiencies)
  • Rod monochromacy (no colour)

Side note:

Although “colour blindness’’ is a commonly used term, it is actually inaccurate. “Colour blindness” means the absence of the ability to detect or distinguish any colour.

C. Blindness

  • Includes very little to no vision.

Side note:

A person is considered legally blind if either:

  • They cannot see at 6m what someone with normal vision can see at 60m.
  • Their field of vision is less than 20 degrees in diameter.

2. Auditory

Auditory disabilities:

  • Hearing loss
  • Hearing impaired
  • Deafness

Side note:

A small percentage of the deaf community that cannot read, and rely on sign language alone.

3. Motor skill

A. Traumatic Injuries

  • Spinal cord injuries that affect upper body
  • Loss or damage to limb(s)

B. Full-body Diseases & Conditions

  • Arthritis
  • Spina Bifida
  • Cerebral Palsy
  • Muscular Dystrophy
  • Multiple Sclerosis
  • Parkinson’s Disease
  • Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS)
  • Locked in syndrome (Cerebromedullospinal disconnection)

C. Transient Conditions

  • Stereotypic Movement Disorder (SMD)
  • Functional Neurological Disorder (FND)
  • Chronic Motor Tick Disorder

D. Arm, Hand and Finger Disorders

  • Osteoarthritis
  • Lymphedema
  • Trigger Finger
  • Buerger's Disease
  • Liebenberg Dyndrome
  • Theumatoid Arthritis
  • Boutonnière Deformity
  • Dupuytren Contracture
  • Chronic Exertional Compartment Syndrome

4. Cognitive

A. Intellectual disabilities

  • Fragile X Syndrome
  • Down Syndrome
  • Autism
  • Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorder (FASD)
  • Apert Syndrome
  • Williams Syndrome
  • Prader-Willi Syndrome (PWS)
  • Phenylketonuria (PKU)
  • Cerebral Palsy

B. Learning disabilities

  • Dyscalculia (maths)
  • Dysgraphia (writing)
  • Dyslexia (reading)
  • Dyspraxia (movement / coordination)
  • Dysphasia (understanding language)
  • Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)
  • Executive Dysfunction Disorder
  • Non-Verbal Learning Disabilities
  • Oral / Written Language Disorders
  • Auditory and visual processing disorders

Disabilities are varied and complex

Many disabilities are spectrums, so people may exhibit mild to extreme symptoms associated with that disability.

Some people have multiple disabilities or conditions such as:

  • A person who is blind and also has hearing loss.
  • A person with Tourettes with Coprolalia, who also has Absence seizures and Functional Neurological Disorder (FND).

Many disabilities change over time such as:

  • Deteriorating eye sight or hearing.
  • Improved functionality after a stroke.
  • Changes due to different treatment or medication.

Making digital products accessible for people with disabilities also benefits others.

For example, some people may experience temporary or situational barriers that are similar to the symptoms of disabilities.

Examples of temporary barriers:

  • A broken finger or an arm in a sling.
  • Temp heading loss.
  • Blurred vision.
  • Concussion.

Examples of situational barriers:

  • Unable to see a screen due to sunlight.
  • Unable to hear video or audio material due to a local noise.
  • Unable to use a mouse while holding a baby.
